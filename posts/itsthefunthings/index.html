It's the Fun Things...

By Will Kennedy
@willkennedyyyy
August 14 2020 | 3 minute read
Tags: inventions stories creativity


An 8-year-old me sat on a lacquered, hardwood floor staring intently at the books haphazardly lined along his family’s bookshelves. Amongst the cookbooks and Emily Bronte novels was stationed a book that captivated my attention “Creating Web Pages with HTML Simplified 2nd Edition’’ by Ruth Maran. I don’t know what about that book made me so transfixed, but, my curiosity was palpable.

I briefly scrutinized the cover and its contents before rushing into my parents’ room. I asked my father about the book and its content. He explained that HTML was a programming language that explained to a computer how it was to structure and display websites and that languages like CSS and Javascript added more styling and interactivity to websites. This answer somewhat dampened my enthusiasm for the book until I asked

“Could you make games with HTML?”

My father faltered and let out an evasive “Ummmmm… it’s not really designed for any of that. But, maybe. I don’t really know”. With that non-committal answer from a man who hadn’t touched the technology in almost a decade, my enthusiasm skyrocketed and I spent the rest of my Saturday reading. That day sparked a passion for programming and STEM that hasn’t dampened for almost 9 years and, I suspect, will persist for my life and be my profession.

Reflecting on that story, my journey through STEM since, and the growing audiences of personalities like Michael Reeves, William Osman, i did a thing, StuffMadeHere and many more has made realise a few things about the STEM engagement and autodidactism, namely, that you should always strive to make it fun.

That statement seems, well, obvious: of course, things are more engaging when they’re entertaining. But, it has been a recurring facet of my, and many of my friends’, journeys in learning to program that there is this notion that learning new concepts or languages or frameworks must be met with this rigorous, academic, and fastidious methodology very reminiscent of structured schooling’s method of teaching - but, that’s not a requirement!

The truth is that the skills and knowledge in STEM can’t just help you accomplish and develop amazing things like space travel, cryptocurrencies, aeroplanes, and so on - it can help you achieve almost anything. You can build everything from the ISS to a Roomba that screams and swears when it bumps into things - and that’s a beautiful and empowering thing. But, the important message in that is to recognise that you can make projects that are fun regardless of your inclinations or background - it’s not all minimax algorithms and futuristic technology.

It can be as stupid and simple as a thing that changes every instance of a word in a text file or something as stupid and as complex as something that stabs tomatoes out of your salad with a big metal piston spike. And that’s something I implore everyone to do - especially if you’re looking to start learning new technologies or you just want to get into STEM recreationally - find a niche that you find incredibly enjoyable and learn whilst you build and explore and smile. Start small and progress to bigger, better, and more wacky projects that push your skills and coerce you to learn new things.

I don’t think I would’ve progressed to where I am if it weren’t for 8-year-old-me’s passion for video games and love of puzzles. I would never be writing this if I’d not serendipitously picked out a book and had my imagination run wild with the fun things I could build if I just learned something new.

    - Will
