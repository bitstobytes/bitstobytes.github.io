Falling for Physics

By Raquel Bradley
@raqueljbradley
August 1 2020 | 2 minute read
Tags: hsc physics education


Throughout my early years of high school, I always intended to take either Biology or Chemistry as my science elective for the HSC. So when I wound up in a class studying Physics due to subject availability, I was not enthusiastic at all. I went in with many preconceptions about physics: that it was all about maths, that you needed to study engineering to understand the content, that my English-loving brain wouldn’t be able to comprehend anything…

Fast forward a year or so and I was regularly at the dinner table, passionately explaining to my family how particle accelerators split atoms into smaller parts (subatomic particles!), how light can be both particles and waves, or how induction cooktops work. Meals were inevitably lengthy.

I had fallen in love with physics. Once I pushed past my initial hesitance, I realised that physics actually helped me make sense of an incredibly and beautifully complex universe. As a naturally inquisitive person, I was always asking “why does that do that?” or “how does that work?”. Physics answered these questions and nurtured further curiosity; I started asking “what does that mean for humanity?” and “how can that be used for good?”.

I learned that maths was part of the subject, but it was there to support propositions and reach important conclusions. The content didn’t always click the first time around, but with teachers and friends who generously answered my endless barrage of questions, I ended up piecing together knowledge that absolutely blew my mind.

I came to realise, too, that as amazing as the technical explanations were, through learning about the people and minds behind these great ideas, I developed an appreciation for where we currently stand in history and all the great developments that have led us to where we are. Electricity, space travel, radiotherapy treatment, the circuits that make up modern technology; all of these were the fruit of hard-working physicians across history, each with their own fascinating stories, struggles and successes.

In the end, my 2 units of Physics didn’t count towards my final ATAR, and even now as I write (having graduated last year) the exact physics behind phenomena have already started to fade. However, I know that my appreciation for the work of physicists and my curiosity about the way the world works grew immensely from my time studying physics – and these things will never fade.

    - Raquel
